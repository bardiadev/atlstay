Buying a short-term rental property is an investment decision that starts with financing — and the loan you choose affects your down payment, your monthly carrying cost, and what the deal actually looks like on paper. The good news is that there are multiple loan structures available to STR buyers. The less-good news is that they each come with different rules, and the wrong choice can create headaches down the road.

This is a general overview to help you understand the landscape before you sit down with a lender. It is not financial or legal advice. Every situation is different — talk to a licensed mortgage professional and a CPA before making financing decisions.

## The Three Most Common Loan Types for STR Buyers

Short-term rental buyers typically encounter three financing structures:

**Conventional investment property loans** — Backed by Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac guidelines, these require the property to be classified as either a primary residence, second home, or investment property. The classification matters because it determines your minimum down payment and rate. Investment property loans (where you won't personally occupy the home at all) generally require a larger down payment than owner-occupied purchases.

**Second-home loans** — If you plan to personally use the property for some portion of the year, a second-home designation may apply. This typically requires that the property be available for your personal use and isn't operated as a full-time rental business. Rates are generally more favorable than pure investment property loans, but the occupancy rules are real — misrepresenting use is mortgage fraud.

**DSCR loans** — Debt Service Coverage Ratio loans qualify you based on the property's income potential rather than your personal income. They've become increasingly popular with real estate investors who own multiple properties or have income structures that don't lend themselves to traditional underwriting. The lender's core question is whether the rental income covers the mortgage payment — if it does (at the required ratio), you may qualify regardless of your personal income.

## How Lenders Think About Short-Term Rental Income

One of the practical complications with STR financing is that lenders handle Airbnb and Vrbo income differently depending on the loan program.

Traditional investment property underwriting often uses appraiser-estimated market rents or a fixed percentage of gross rents — not your Airbnb projections. That can make conventional investment property qualification tricky if the long-term rental rate in the area is lower than what the property can realistically earn as a short-term rental.

DSCR lenders are more likely to use STR-specific income data: actual income history from the platform if the property is already operating, or market-rate STR comparables for a new purchase. This is one reason some STR investors find DSCR products more practical — the underwriting actually reflects how the property will perform.

## Key Factors That Affect Your Rate and Down Payment

| Factor | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Property classification | Primary, second home, or investment each have different rate and down payment floors |
| Loan-to-value ratio | Lower LTV (bigger down payment) generally improves your rate |
| Credit score | Affects rate significantly, especially for investment property loans |
| Reserves | Many lenders require 6–12 months of reserves for investment properties |
| Rental income documentation | DSCR lenders want clear income history or credible comparable data |

## Due Diligence Before You Close

Financing is only one layer of pre-purchase due diligence for a short-term rental. Before you commit to a property, two non-financing items deserve as much attention as your loan terms:

**HOA and condo rules.** Many condo associations and planned communities prohibit short-term rentals outright, or impose minimum lease periods (often 30 or 90 days) that effectively prevent Airbnb use. These restrictions live in the CC&Rs — read them, not just the listing description. A property that can't legally operate as an STR under its HOA rules is a problem no lender can solve.

**Local regulations.** Short-term rental permitting varies by city and county. Atlanta has a licensing framework, and other Georgia municipalities have their own rules. Understanding what's required — and what it costs — before closing is part of responsible underwriting. ## Working the Numbers Before You Apply

Before approaching a lender, it helps to have a realistic picture of what the property can earn. Lenders who use STR income data in their underwriting will want something credible — not a best-case-scenario projection but a comps-based estimate grounded in what comparable properties in the same area are actually producing.

That same projection is useful for your own investment analysis: if the realistic income range doesn't cover your debt service and operating expenses with comfortable margin, the deal may not work regardless of how attractive the purchase price looks. ## Talking to the Right Lender

Not all lenders are equally comfortable with short-term rental purchases. A lender who hasn't worked with STR investors before may default to the most restrictive classification even when better options exist. Ask specifically:

- Do you offer DSCR loans, and how do you handle STR income?
- How do you classify a property that will be rented on Airbnb full-time?
- What documentation do you require for projected or historical rental income?

Getting those answers before you submit an application saves time and avoids surprises at the closing table.
