*This article is general information only — not legal, tax, or regulatory advice. Short-term rental rules change frequently. Confirm current requirements directly with the city or county that governs your property before listing.*

If you own — or are thinking about buying — a short-term rental in Georgia, the single most important thing to understand is that there is no one set of rules. Georgia regulates short-term rentals locally, so the requirements for a cabin in the North Georgia mountains, a Historic District rowhouse in Savannah, an intown Atlanta condo, and a suburban metro home can all be meaningfully different. This guide explains the framework that ties them together, so you can figure out which questions to ask and who to ask them of.

We do not quote specific permit fees, tax rates, or ordinance numbers anywhere in this guide — those details change, vary by jurisdiction, and stating them wrong is worse than not stating them. What you get instead is the structure, plus links to the specific markets and counties we cover, each pointing you to the correct local authority.

## There Is No Statewide STR License — Regulation Is Local

Georgia has not adopted a statewide short-term rental licensing law that overrides local control. The state's role shows up mainly in taxation — state sales tax, administered by the Georgia Department of Revenue, and the enabling of local lodging taxes. The rules that determine whether and how you can operate — permitting, zoning, occupancy, conduct — are set by individual cities and counties.

That is why two properties a few miles apart can face very different requirements, and why the most common and most expensive mistake Georgia owners make is assuming that what they read about one city applies to their own. It frequently does not.

## The Five Layers Almost Every Georgia Owner Must Check

No matter where in Georgia your property sits, five layers tend to apply. Working through them in order is the reliable way to get compliant:

| Layer | What it determines | Who to ask |
|---|---|---|
| Jurisdiction | Whether a city or the county governs your address | Property tax records; the city and county directly |
| Zoning | Whether STR use is allowed at that location | The governing city or county |
| Registration / permitting | Authorization to operate before listing | The governing city or county |
| Taxes | State sales tax + local lodging tax obligations | Georgia DOR; city/county; a CPA |
| HOA / covenants | Whether your community independently prohibits STRs | Your HOA or condo association |

Each layer is independent. Passing one does not exempt you from another — a permit does not override an HOA prohibition, and favorable zoning does not eliminate your tax obligations.

### 1. Jurisdiction: City or County?

A property inside an incorporated city's limits is generally governed by that city; a property in an unincorporated area is governed by the county. Some areas use a consolidated city-county government (Athens-Clarke County is the clearest example). This sounds simple, but it routinely trips owners up — many cabins marketed as "Blue Ridge" or "Helen" actually sit in unincorporated Fannin or White County, not inside those small city limits. Settle jurisdiction with property records and direct confirmation, not a mailing address.

### 2. Zoning: Is the Use Allowed Here?

Most Georgia jurisdictions treat short-term rentals differently across zoning districts, and eligibility can depend on the specific location and how the property is classified (owner-occupied versus non-owner-occupied is a common distinction). Strong rental demand at an address does not mean the use is permitted there. Confirm zoning eligibility before you invest.

### 3. Registration and Permitting

Many Georgia cities and counties now require a permit or registration before you list; others rely primarily on zoning enforcement. Tourism-heavy and high-density markets tend to have the most developed permitting systems. Complete the local process **before** accepting bookings — operating without a required permit can mean fines and forced delisting.

### 4. Occupancy and Hotel-Motel (Lodging) Tax

Short-term rentals in Georgia generally owe Georgia state sales tax plus a local hotel-motel (lodging) excise tax, and some jurisdictions add their own fees. Platforms collect and remit some of these in some places, but coverage varies and changes. Confirm what your platform covers, what you must file directly, and the current rates with the Georgia Department of Revenue, your city or county, and a licensed CPA. Our [tax overview](/resources/short-term-rental-taxes-atlanta-owners/) explains the categories. This is general information, not tax advice.

### 5. HOA and Condo Covenants

Associations frequently restrict or prohibit short-term rentals through covenants and bylaws, independently of government permits. This is one of the most overlooked layers, and it can stop a listing cold. Read your governing documents and confirm with the association — our [HOA rules guide](/resources/hoa-rules-and-airbnb-atlanta/) explains how.

## How It Varies: Tourism Towns vs. Metro vs. Mountains

While the five layers are universal, the **intensity** of regulation varies by market type:

- **Established tourism cities** (such as Savannah) tend to have the most detailed, location-specific ordinances, including permitted zones and density considerations.
- **Mountain cabin markets** (such as Blue Ridge / Fannin County and Helen / White County) increasingly attach occupancy, septic, and life-safety expectations as volumes grow.
- **Metro Atlanta** is a patchwork: the City of Atlanta runs a developed licensing program, DeKalb County has added county-level requirements, and surrounding counties and cities each handle short-term rentals their own way — some through registration, others mainly through zoning.

## A Reliable Verification Sequence for Any Georgia Property

Run the five layers in order: confirm jurisdiction with property records and direct contact; confirm zoning eligibility and any owner-occupancy classification at the specific address; complete permitting or registration before listing; settle tax obligations with the state, the local government, and a CPA (knowing what your platform remits); and review HOA and condo covenants, which can prohibit short-term rentals regardless of government rules. Then re-verify at least annually — Georgia's local rules change often, and a market that was permissive last year may not be this year.
