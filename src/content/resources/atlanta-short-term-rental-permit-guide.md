Running a short-term rental in the City of Atlanta requires more than creating a listing — it requires a valid permit issued by the City before you accept your first guest. That's not unique to Atlanta; most major U.S. cities now regulate the short-term rental industry. But Atlanta's framework has specific requirements worth understanding before you invest in furnishing a property or committing to a lease.

This post is a general orientation to how the permitting process works. It is not legal or regulatory advice. Requirements can and do change, and the authoritative source for current rules is always the City of Atlanta itself.

## Why the City Requires a Permit

Atlanta's short-term rental ordinance exists to address a set of concerns the City has treated as legitimate: housing availability, neighborhood character, safety standards, and tax compliance. Whatever your view of those policy goals, the practical reality is that the licensing requirement is enforced and non-compliance carries real consequences — fines, listing removal, and in repeat cases, more significant penalties.

The permit requirement also serves as a mechanism for the City to ensure that STR operators are collecting and remitting the applicable hotel-motel and other occupancy taxes. Those tax obligations are separate from the permit but connected to it in that the City uses the licensing process to bring operators into the formal tax collection system. For a full orientation to the regulatory landscape, our [Atlanta short-term rental regulations guide](/resources/atlanta-short-term-rental-regulations/) provides more detail on both permit and tax considerations.

## The General Steps in the Permit Process

While the specific requirements are subject to change and must be verified with the City, the general shape of the application process has historically followed these stages:

**1. Confirm your property's eligibility.** Before applying, verify that your property's zoning classification permits short-term rental use, and check whether you're in a condo or HOA community with its own restrictions. Neither of these is the City's concern — they're your due diligence.

**2. Determine the applicable permit category.** Atlanta's ordinance has distinguished between owner-occupied rentals (where the host lives on-site) and non-owner-occupied rentals (entire-unit rentals without the host present). These categories have carried different rules and, historically, different permit availability. Confirm which category applies to your property and what the current rules for that category are.

**3. Gather required documentation.** Applications typically require proof of identity, proof of property ownership or authorization, proof of residency (for owner-occupied categories), and acknowledgment of applicable rules. The specific document list should come from the City's current application checklist.

**4. Submit your application and pay applicable fees.** Applications go through the City of Atlanta's designated licensing or building office. Fee amounts are set by the City and are subject to change — do not rely on any dollar figure you find in a blog post, including this one.

**5. Await review and approval.** Processing times vary. The City may request additional information. Don't list your property or accept bookings until you have a permit number in hand.

**6. Display your permit number on your listing.** Most platforms require hosts to enter a license or registration number. Atlanta specifically requires it, and listing without one is a violation.

## Taxes: A Separate but Connected Obligation

A short-term rental permit is not the same as tax registration. Operating with a permit does not automatically mean you're compliant with your occupancy tax obligations — those are separate filings with separate agencies.

Short-term rentals in Atlanta are generally subject to state hotel-motel tax, city hotel-motel tax, and potentially county-level taxes depending on whether your property is in Fulton or DeKalb County. Some platforms collect and remit certain taxes on behalf of hosts; others do not, or only do so for some jurisdictions.

The specific tax rates, who collects what, and how to file are details that change and that vary by your exact situation. Verify your tax obligations with the Georgia Department of Revenue, the City of Atlanta Revenue Department, and your county — or work with a licensed CPA who handles short-term rental operators in Georgia. This blog post is general information and is not tax advice.

## Common Pitfalls Before You Apply

Several avoidable issues come up repeatedly for Atlanta hosts going through the permit process for the first time:

- **HOA conflicts.** The City may issue you a permit even if your HOA prohibits STRs. Those are parallel systems, not one that overrides the other. Read your governing documents — or have an attorney read them — before you apply.
- **Zoning assumptions.** Being in a residential neighborhood doesn't automatically mean STRs are permitted in your specific zone. Confirm zoning eligibility before investing in setup costs.
- **Timing.** Permit processing takes time. Starting the application after you've already committed to launch costs is a common source of stress that's entirely avoidable with earlier planning.
- **Relying on outdated information.** Atlanta's STR ordinance has been updated multiple times since the City first adopted it. What was true two years ago may not be true today. Always go to the City's official resources for current requirements.

## What to Verify Directly with the City of Atlanta

| Topic | Where to verify |
|---|---|

| Zoning compliance for your address | City of Atlanta Office of Zoning and Development |
| Application fees and processing times | City of Atlanta licensing office directly |
| Hotel-motel tax rates and filing | City of Atlanta Revenue Department; Georgia Dept. of Revenue |
| County-level tax obligations | Fulton or DeKalb County Revenue / Tax Commissioner |
