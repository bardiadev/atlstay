*This article is general information only — not insurance advice. Coverage needs vary by property, use, and individual circumstances. Always consult a licensed insurance agent familiar with short-term rental properties before making coverage decisions.*

Most Atlanta homeowners who start listing on Airbnb or Vrbo focus on the platform setup, the listing, and the first few bookings. Insurance tends to be an afterthought — until something goes wrong. A guest slips on a wet deck. A bathtub overflow causes water damage downstairs. A storm makes the property temporarily unusable. Whether your insurance actually responds in any of those moments depends on whether your coverage was structured for short-term rental use. For most hosts, a standard homeowners policy is not.

## Why Standard Homeowners Insurance Often Doesn't Cover STR Activity

A standard homeowners insurance policy is underwritten for a specific use case: the owner lives in the home, guests are visitors, and the property is not being operated as a commercial accommodation business. The moment you accept payment from guests in exchange for stays, the nature of the risk changes in ways that many standard policies were explicitly designed to exclude.

Two provisions are common sources of problems:

**Business activity exclusions.** Many policies exclude coverage for losses arising from business activity conducted on the premises. Insurers have argued — and in some cases, courts have agreed — that operating a short-term rental qualifies as a business activity. If a claim arises during an active guest booking and your insurer determines it falls under this exclusion, the claim may be denied.

**Vacancy and occupancy conditions.** Some policies impose conditions around how long a property can be unoccupied or impose restrictions on non-owner occupancy. Depending on how your property is used and how the policy language is written, extended periods between bookings or continuous guest use may create complications.

The safest path is to call your current insurer, describe how the property is being used, and ask directly whether your existing policy covers short-term rental activity. Get that answer in writing.

## What Platform Host Protection Does — and Doesn't Do

Airbnb offers a host protection program called AirCover. Vrbo and other platforms have similar programs. These are meaningful benefits, and they can help in some guest-damage scenarios. They are not insurance.

Here is what that distinction matters in practice:

| Aspect | Your own STR insurance | Platform host protection |
|---|---|---|
| Who controls the policy | You, through your agent | The platform, under its own terms |
| Scope of coverage | Defined by your policy, including liability | Platform-defined; subject to platform exclusions |
| Claims process | Through your insurer | Through the platform's resolution center |
| Continuity | As long as you pay premiums | Subject to change by the platform |
| Legal enforceability | Insurance contract | Platform agreement |

Platform terms change, claims processes can be slow, and the exclusions in these programs can be extensive. Most licensed insurance professionals advise treating platform coverage as a supplementary backstop — not your primary line of protection.

## The Categories of Coverage Worth Understanding

When you speak with a licensed agent about short-term rental insurance, these are the main categories to discuss:

**Property damage.** Coverage for physical damage to the structure and its contents during guest stays — not just accidental damage, but also scenarios where the cause of loss is less clear-cut. Consider what you have in the property: furnishings, electronics, appliances. A well-equipped short-term rental represents real value that a standard contents policy may not cover if the loss occurs during a booking.

**Liability.** If a guest is injured on your property and sues, liability coverage pays your legal defense costs and any damages up to policy limits. This is arguably the most important category — a serious personal injury claim can be financially significant regardless of whether the injury was your fault. Standard homeowners liability may exclude guest-injury scenarios in a rental context.

**Loss of income.** If a covered event — fire, major water damage, storm damage — makes your property uninhabitable for a period, loss of income (also called business interruption or loss of rents) coverage replaces the rental income you would have earned during repairs. Without this, a property off-market during a busy season can be a double loss.

**Premises liability beyond guests.** Liability coverage should extend to anyone lawfully on the property, including delivery personnel, cleaning crews, or maintenance workers who might be injured.

## Primary Residence vs. Investment Property

How a property is used affects what type of policy is appropriate. A host who rents their primary residence for occasional short trips while traveling is in a fundamentally different underwriting situation than an investor who owns a dedicated STR property they never occupy. Many insurers have different products for these scenarios, and the right coverage depends on the accurate picture.

When you speak with an agent, be precise: is this your primary home? How many nights per year do you rent? Is it rented year-round or seasonally? Do you stay there yourself? These facts drive the underwriting, and misrepresenting them — intentionally or not — is the mechanism by which claims get denied after the fact.

## Finding the Right Coverage

Several insurers now offer policies specifically designed for short-term rental properties, either as standalone products or as endorsements to existing landlord or homeowners policies. These products have expanded significantly as the STR industry has grown, and coverage quality varies.

The right way to find appropriate coverage is to work with a licensed insurance agent who has experience with short-term rental properties — not to shop for the cheapest policy and hope it covers everything. An agent who understands STR exposures will ask the right questions, explain what each policy does and doesn't cover, and help you understand the gaps before you need to file a claim.
