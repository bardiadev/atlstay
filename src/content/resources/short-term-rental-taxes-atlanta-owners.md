*This article is general information only — not legal, tax, or accounting advice. Tax rules change. Always verify current requirements with the City of Atlanta, your county government, and a licensed CPA before filing or collecting taxes on your rental income.*

Renting your Atlanta property on Airbnb or Vrbo comes with real tax obligations that go beyond what you might owe on a long-term lease. Understanding the categories of taxes involved — before you receive your first booking payout — puts you in a far better position than discovering them at year-end. This guide walks through the main tax categories Atlanta short-term rental owners encounter and explains where to go for the specifics.

## The Three Main Tax Categories to Know

Atlanta-area STR owners generally encounter taxes in three distinct buckets:

1. **Lodging or hotel-motel excise tax** — A local tax on the rental of short-term accommodations, imposed at the city and/or county level. This is typically charged on top of the base rental rate.
2. **State and local sales tax on accommodations** — Georgia levies a state sales tax, and local jurisdictions add their own rates on top of that. Rental accommodations are taxable under Georgia law.
3. **Federal and state income tax** — Rental income is generally taxable income. How it's treated depends on your overall tax situation, how much you use the property personally, and other factors.

These categories interact with each other and with your personal tax situation in ways that make general rates impossible to give accurately here — and counterproductive even if we could, because they change. A licensed CPA with short-term rental experience is the right person to work through your specific picture.

## How Platform Tax Collection Works — and Where the Gaps Are

Airbnb and Vrbo have negotiated tax collection and remittance agreements with many Georgia jurisdictions. Under these agreements, the platform collects certain taxes from guests at booking and sends them directly to the relevant tax authority — theoretically reducing the host's burden.

The complication: these agreements vary by platform, change over time, and may not cover every applicable tax in every Georgia county. Your responsibility as a host is to understand which taxes your platform covers and which you remain responsible for collecting and remitting yourself.

| Tax type | May be covered by platform? | Your action |
|---|---|---|
| State sales tax on accommodations | Often, but verify | Confirm with platform and state |
| City/county lodging excise tax | Sometimes, jurisdiction-dependent | Confirm with city/county and platform |
| Federal income tax on rental income | No — platforms report payouts, not taxes | Report on your federal return |
| Georgia state income tax | No | Report on your Georgia return |

Do not assume platform coverage equals full compliance. The City of Atlanta and surrounding counties have their own registration and remittance requirements — confirm the current status directly with those agencies and with your CPA.

## The Federal 14-Day Rule: A Concept Worth Understanding

The IRS has a general provision — widely referenced as the "14-day rule" or "vacation home rule" — that affects how rental income and deductible expenses are calculated when you also use the property personally for part of the year. The rule draws a line between a property treated as a rental business and one treated as a personal residence for tax purposes.

The actual implications depend on your specific use patterns, how you file, and your individual circumstances. It's a meaningful planning point for anyone who might stay in their own listing occasionally. This is a conversation to have with a CPA — the general concept is useful to know, but the application requires someone who knows your full tax situation.

## Deductions and Record-Keeping

One of the practical advantages of operating a short-term rental as a business is the range of potentially deductible expenses: property management fees, platform commissions, supplies, cleaning services, repairs, depreciation, and others. The list of what qualifies — and how to document it — depends on your filing approach.

Good record-keeping from day one makes year-end far less painful. Most STR owners working with a [property management company](/services/) find that management fees, cleaning invoices, and maintenance records are automatically documented through the management process, which simplifies tax preparation.

Keep payout summaries from your platform, receipts for all expenses related to the property, and any correspondence about repairs or capital improvements. Your CPA will guide you on depreciation schedules and anything specific to your situation.

## Registering and Staying Current

Before your first guest arrival, the City of Atlanta requires short-term rental hosts to hold a valid permit. The permitting and tax registration process can overlap — and both carry compliance requirements. Our [Atlanta short-term rental regulations guide](/resources/atlanta-short-term-rental-regulations/) explains the permit process in detail and is a good starting point before you call the city.

Tax rules don't stay static. Rates get adjusted, new jurisdictions add requirements, and platform agreements change. The safest approach is to verify your obligations directly with the relevant government agencies at least once per year, and any time you hear about potential rule changes.
