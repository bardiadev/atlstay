Running out of toilet paper at 11 p.m. on a Saturday is a one-star review waiting to happen. Running out of trash bags at 8 a.m. before a same-day check-out is a cleaning team scrambling instead of turning the unit. Both are avoidable — and the solution isn't checking in with your cleaner before every stay. It's a par-level system.

A par-level system is a structured way to manage consumable inventory so that restocking happens on a predictable schedule rather than in reaction to shortages. Hotels have used this logic for decades. For Airbnb operators running one property or ten, adapting it to short-term rentals is one of the highest-leverage operational improvements you can make.

## What Par Level Actually Means

"Par" is a hospitality industry term for the minimum quantity of a supply that should be on hand at any given time. When stock drops to par, you reorder — before it reaches zero.

For short-term rentals, par levels account for the fact that your property turns over continuously, often with same-day or back-to-back bookings that leave no window for a supply run. Setting a par at one full stay's worth of consumables is too thin. A safer standard is one-and-a-half to two stays' worth in the on-site supply cache at all times, so a missed delivery or an unexpected long booking doesn't strand your guests or your cleaner.

## Build Your Supply Categories First

Before you set quantities, you need a complete category list. The standard Airbnb consumable categories are:

| Category | Core items |
|---|---|
| Bathroom | Toilet paper, hand soap, shampoo, conditioner, body wash, facial tissues |
| Kitchen | Dish soap, sponge, paper towels, trash bags, garbage can liners, coffee and filters or pods |
| Laundry | Detergent pods or liquid, dryer sheets or wool balls |
| Safety & maintenance | Replacement batteries (AA, AAA, 9V), spare bulbs for every fixture type, extra HVAC filters |
| Seasonal additions | Extra AC filters (summer), allergy relief packets, sunscreen packets (warm months) |

Start with this list, walk your property, and add anything specific to your setup — a fireplace means lighter fluid or fire starters; a hot tub means testing strips and chemical tabs. The goal is a written inventory that covers every consumable a guest or cleaner might need.

## Setting Par Quantities Per Item

Once you have your category list, assign a par quantity to each item. Ask: how much of this does a typical guest party use during an average stay? Then multiply by two to arrive at the on-site minimum.

Toilet paper is the obvious example: if a two-bedroom unit hosting four guests for a long weekend typically goes through two to three rolls per bathroom, your on-site minimum per bathroom should be four to six rolls. When a turnover brings that count below par, replenishment gets triggered — not when the last roll is gone.

The same logic applies to less-obvious items. Dish soap is easy to overlook; guests doing dishes across a four-day stay can empty a small bottle. Coffee pods get used fast in units marketed to business travelers. Trash bags disappear in large-group bookings. Each of those should have a par number set based on your actual guest mix, not a generic guess.

## The On-Site Supply Cabinet

A par-level system only works if stock is physically at the property and organized in a way your cleaning team can assess at a glance. A dedicated supply cabinet — a locked utility closet, a section of a laundry room, or a labeled storage area under a bathroom sink — is essential for anything beyond a single unit.

The cabinet should be organized by category with clear labels, ideally with a physical count visible or a simple checklist posted inside the door. Your cleaner should be able to run a five-minute inventory check at the end of each turnover and mark what needs replenishment. They flag it; restocking happens before the next booking.

## Procurement: Ending the Ad Hoc Shopping Trip

The biggest failure mode for individual hosts managing their own supplies is reactive purchasing — you realize you're out of something, you make a trip or place a rush order, and you pay a premium for the urgency. At scale, this is both expensive and time-consuming.

The practical alternatives:

**Subscription delivery.** Set a recurring order through a warehouse club or consumer delivery service for your highest-volume items — paper goods, trash bags, soap. Tune the frequency after your first few months of actual usage data.

**Commercial supply sourcing.** For properties running at high occupancy, commercial-grade suppliers often offer better per-unit pricing than consumer retail, especially for bulk paper goods and cleaning consumables.

**Centralized storage.** If you have multiple Atlanta properties, a single storage unit near your portfolio eliminates per-property delivery complexity and lets you buy at genuine bulk prices.

## Tracking and Adjusting Over Time

Set your par levels based on reasonable estimates and your first few months will tell you where you're over- or understocked. Paper towels going through faster than expected? Raise the par. Conditioner sitting untouched because you're running a short-stay property where most guests don't wash hair? Drop it or eliminate it.

The system improves with actual usage data. A simple spreadsheet tracking what each turnover uses, over a few months, gives you a real picture of consumption patterns — and lets you adjust par quantities before shortages happen rather than after.
