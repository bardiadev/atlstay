Running an Airbnb involves real operating costs — and the tax code acknowledges that. Short-term rental hosts who track their expenses carefully and work with a qualified tax professional are in a position to reduce their taxable income by claiming the deductions available to them. Understanding the general categories is the starting point.

This post is an educational overview of the types of expenses that are commonly deductible for short-term rental hosts. It is not tax advice, and your specific situation — how often you rent, whether you also use the property, your overall income picture — determines how the rules apply to you. Always work with a licensed CPA who has experience with short-term rental properties.

## Why Deductions Matter for Short-Term Rental Owners

The IRS generally treats short-term rental income as business income when you provide services to guests, which means you're subject to self-employment tax rules but also entitled to deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses. Getting this right requires both knowing which categories apply and keeping the documentation to back them up.

The categories below are a starting framework. Some of them require proration if you also use the property for personal stays. Others involve depreciation schedules that span multiple years. The complexity is manageable — but it rewards having a professional in your corner.

## Cleaning and Turnover Costs

Cleaning is one of the most straightforward and frequently incurred expenses in short-term rental operations. Fees paid to a cleaning service, or the cost of cleaning supplies you purchase yourself, are deductible as ordinary business expenses.

If you handle cleaning personally, you cannot deduct a wage for your own time — but any products and supplies you buy are deductible. For hosts who outsource turnover to a cleaning crew, keeping invoices and payment records for every clean is the simplest documentation practice.

## Supplies and Consumables

The items guests use during their stay — toiletries, paper products, coffee, kitchen staples — are deductible as supplies. So are linens, towels, and any small household items you replace or replenish between guests.

Larger items purchased for guest use, like kitchen equipment or electronics, may fall under a different treatment depending on their cost and useful life. When in doubt, flag these for your CPA rather than assuming.

## Professional Management Fees

If you use a property management company to handle your short-term rental, the fees you pay for that service are generally deductible. This includes ongoing management fees, any booking assistance fees, and fees for specific services like maintenance coordination or guest communication.

Professional management fees are a clear, documented expense — you'll receive statements or invoices from your manager that serve directly as tax records. ## Utilities and Operating Costs

Utility costs for a short-term rental property — electricity, gas, water, internet, cable — are deductible. If the property is used exclusively as a rental, the full amount is generally deductible. If you mix personal use with rental use, the deductible portion is typically prorated based on rental days.

Other operating costs that commonly apply:

| Expense Category | Common Examples |
|---|---|
| Utilities | Electricity, gas, water, internet |
| Platform fees | Airbnb / Vrbo service fees charged to hosts |
| Supplies | Toiletries, paper goods, coffee, cleaning products |
| Professional services | CPA fees, legal fees related to the rental |
| Insurance | Rental property or STR-specific insurance premiums |
| Marketing | Photography, listing upgrades, advertising |
| Software and subscriptions | Pricing tools, property management software |

## Repairs and Maintenance

Costs to keep your property in its current working condition are generally deductible in the year they occur. This includes fixing appliances, patching damage, repainting, and routine maintenance work.

The key distinction is between a repair (maintaining current condition) and a capital improvement (adding to or significantly upgrading the property). Capital improvements are not deducted immediately — they're added to the property's basis and recovered through depreciation over time. The distinction matters and is worth confirming with your CPA for any larger project.

## Depreciation

Depreciation is one of the most significant deductions available to rental property owners, and one of the most often overlooked. The IRS allows property owners to deduct the cost of their building (not the land) over a designated recovery period. Furnishings and appliances may also be depreciable on their own schedule.

Depreciation is a non-cash deduction — meaning you're deducting the wear and cost of the asset over time without actually spending that money in the current year. Getting it right requires proper asset classification and basis calculation. If you've owned a rental property for years without tracking depreciation, a CPA can often help you catch up.

## Vehicle and Travel Expenses

Driving to your property for inspections, picking up supplies, meeting with contractors — these trips may be deductible as business mileage. Hosts who use their personal vehicle for rental-related tasks have two options: deduct actual vehicle expenses (proration required if the car is also used personally) or use the standard mileage rate and keep a mileage log.

Travel to the property for management purposes — not personal stays — is the line that matters here. Keep records contemporaneously, as reconstructing mileage after the fact is harder to defend.

## Keep Records Throughout the Year

The cleanest way to claim deductions confidently is to maintain records in real time rather than reconstructing them at tax season. A simple system — a folder for receipts, a log for mileage, statements from your management company — goes a long way toward making your CPA's job easier and your return defensible.
