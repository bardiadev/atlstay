Savannah is one of the most naturally suited cities in Georgia for short-term rental hosting. Its combination of year-round tourism, iconic walkable streetscapes, and a guest base that actively seeks out the experience of staying in a historic home rather than a hotel creates demand conditions that few other Georgia markets can match.

But Savannah also has some of the strictest short-term rental regulations in the state. Understanding both sides — the strong demand and the regulatory framework — is essential before listing your property here.

## Why Savannah Draws Visitors Year-Round

Most tourism markets in Georgia have a clear peak and a clear off-season. Savannah is different. The city's Historic District, with its 22 squares, moss-draped live oaks, and one of the largest intact collections of historic architecture in the United States, draws visitors in every month. River Street, the waterfront dining and entertainment corridor, and the dining scene built around James Beard-recognized restaurants add layers of draw beyond the architecture.

SCAD — the Savannah College of Art and Design — brings families for move-in, graduation, and family weekends throughout the academic year. The city's food and beverage scene has grown substantially in recent years, drawing culinary travelers. And Savannah's reputation as one of the country's top bachelorette party destinations creates a steady flow of weekend group bookings that helps fill the calendar in periods that might otherwise be slow.

The St. Patrick's Day parade, one of the largest in the United States, is Savannah's single biggest demand event of the year — properties near the Historic District during that week can command rates that far exceed any other time of year.

## The Savannah Guest Profile

Savannah attracts a notably different traveler than Atlanta. The draw here is overwhelmingly experiential — guests come specifically for the city itself, not to pass through it. That creates a guest base that tends to be more engaged with the property, more likely to spend on dining and experiences, and more likely to leave detailed reviews.

Key guest segments:

| Guest type | What drives them |
|---|---|
| Couples on getaways | Romance, architecture, dining — Savannah delivers all three |
| Bachelorette / bachelor groups | One of the top destinations nationally for this segment |
| History and heritage travelers | NAACP heritage tourism, Civil War history, architectural tours |
| SCAD family visitors | Academic calendar creates predictable demand spikes |
| Culinary travelers | Savannah's restaurant scene is a destination in its own right |
| Event attendees | St. Patrick's Day, Savannah Film Festival, music events |

Properties that can accommodate groups — three or more bedrooms — are especially well-positioned for the bachelorette and bachelor party market, which tends to book well in advance and pay premium rates for the right property.

## Savannah's STR Ordinance: Read This Before Listing

This is where Savannah differs fundamentally from other Georgia markets, including Atlanta. **Savannah has a distinct, stricter short-term rental ordinance with designated permit zones. You cannot assume that Atlanta's rules, Chatham County's rules, or any other Georgia city's rules apply here.**

Key points every prospective Savannah host needs to understand:

- **Permitted zones matter.** Savannah has defined areas where short-term rentals are allowed. Operating outside a permitted zone — regardless of how good your listing looks — puts you at legal and financial risk.
- **Owner-occupied vs. non-owner-occupied rules differ.** Savannah's ordinance has historically treated these property categories differently, with different eligibility, caps, and requirements for each.
- **Permits are required.** You must obtain a City of Savannah short-term rental permit before listing. Operating without one exposes you to fines and forced delisting.
- **Rules are actively enforced and subject to change.** Savannah has been one of the more proactive Georgia cities in enforcing STR regulations, and the ordinance has evolved over time.

Before you list — or even before you invest in a property to use as a short-term rental — verify your specific address and property type against the **current** City of Savannah STR ordinance. Contact the City of Savannah's Zoning Department or Revenue Division directly to confirm eligibility, permit requirements, and applicable restrictions. Do not rely on secondhand accounts or older versions of the rules.

This is a genuinely important step, not a formality. The consequences of non-compliance in Savannah are real.

## What Makes a Savannah Property Stand Out

Once you've confirmed your property can legally operate as a short-term rental, the positioning question becomes: what makes guests choose your listing over the dozens of others in the Historic District?

Character matters enormously here. Guests who travel to Savannah specifically for the city's aesthetic are drawn to properties that reflect it — original hardwood floors, period architectural details, thoughtful design that complements rather than fights the building's history. A generic, corporate-feeling interior in a Savannah rowhouse is a missed opportunity.

Walkability and location within the Historic District are the other major differentiators. Proximity to the squares, River Street, and the main restaurant corridors directly affects your ability to command premium rates. Guests want to walk to dinner.

## Pricing Savannah's Demand Peaks

Savannah's seasonality has distinct peaks that reward dynamic pricing. Spring — particularly the weeks around St. Patrick's Day — is the most intense demand period of the year. Fall is strong as well, driven by comfortable weather, fall festivals, and the SCAD calendar. Summer brings heat but also consistent leisure volume from families and long-weekend travelers.

The bachelorette market tends to book well in advance for specific popular weekends (Valentine's Day adjacent, graduation weekends, New Year's Eve). Building these into your pricing calendar well ahead of time — rather than adjusting reactively — captures the full upside.
