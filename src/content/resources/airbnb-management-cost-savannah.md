Short-term rental management in Savannah typically runs anywhere from roughly 15% to 40% of gross booking revenue, depending on how much the company actually does. In a market this seasonal and event-driven, what you pay matters far less than what that fee buys you. This guide breaks down how Savannah management pricing works, what should be included, and how to tell whether a given rate is genuinely worth it.

## How Savannah Airbnb Management Pricing Works

Almost every manager charges a percentage of **gross booking revenue** — the full amount a guest pays before platform fees. A minority quote against **net revenue** (after Airbnb's cut). Those are very different numbers, so confirm the basis before comparing two quotes side by side.

The percentage itself is driven mostly by service depth. A company handling every part of operations — pricing, guests, cleaning, maintenance — sits at the higher end. A "co-host" arrangement where you still coordinate local turnovers sits lower. Savannah adds a wrinkle most metro markets don't: a high volume of historic properties with specific upkeep needs, and a calendar built around a handful of make-or-break weekends.

## What a Full-Service Fee Should Include

For a Savannah property, a genuine full-service manager should bundle all of the following without add-on charges:

- **Listing and marketing** — professional photography, optimized copy across Airbnb, VRBO, and Booking.com, and ongoing seasonal updates
- **Event-calibrated pricing** — dynamic rates tuned to St. Patrick's Day weekend, the Savannah Food & Wine Festival, SCAD events, and wedding season, with minimum-night rules set well in advance
- **Guest management** — inquiry screening, 24/7 communication, keyless or coordinated check-in, and review handling
- **Operations** — vetted local cleaning teams who understand historic homes, linen and supply restocking, and maintenance coordination
- **Reporting** — monthly owner statements and tax documentation

If any of those are missing — or billed separately — that's not really full-service.

## Full-Service vs. Cheap Base Rates

A low headline rate is tempting until you see what's excluded. The most common pattern is a base percentage in the 10–15% range with separate charges layered on top.

| Service Level | Typical Range | What's Included |
|---|---|---|
| Full-service management | 18–35% | Listing, event pricing, guests, cleaning, maintenance — everything |
| Partial / co-hosting | 10–18% | Listing and pricing; owner runs local operations |
| Listing-only | 5–10% | Setup only |

In Savannah, the operational burden you'd carry under a partial model is heavier than in a quieter suburb. Coordinating a same-day turnover on a carriage house between two River Street weekends is not a casual side task. For owners who want genuinely passive income, full-service is the only model that delivers it.

## How to Evaluate Whether a Manager Is Worth It

The math is more straightforward than it looks. Compare your realistic self-managed scenario — occupancy times nightly rate times available nights, minus your time, cleaning, and platform costs — against a managed scenario where professional pricing and photography typically lift occupancy and average rate. In a peak-driven market like Savannah, the swing on a single mispriced St. Patrick's Day weekend can exceed a full year of management fees.

Then factor in your time. If you don't want midnight lockout calls during a packed festival weekend, the fee is the price of that freedom. Ask any prospective manager how they handle event pricing, what their turnover process looks like for historic properties, and exactly what their all-in rate excludes.

## Savannah's Market: Why Active Management Pays Off

Savannah is one of the South's most resilient travel destinations, and its demand profile rewards attentive management. The anchor is St. Patrick's Day weekend — among the largest celebrations of its kind in the country — which fills every room in the city and pushes rates to their annual ceiling. Spring and fall are both strong shoulder seasons, propped up by pleasant weather, SCAD events, the Savannah Food & Wine Festival, and the Book Festival. Layer in hundreds of destination weddings a year and a steady leisure stream drawn to River Street, Forsyth Park, and the historic squares, and you get a calendar with demand almost year-round.

That richness is exactly why under-management is so costly here. A property that misses the St. Patrick's window, sets foliage-adjacent fall rates too late, or mishandles minimum nights during a wedding weekend leaves its biggest revenue events on the table. Compliance adds another layer: short-term rental rules in Savannah vary by zoning district, and Chatham County has separate requirements for unincorporated areas. A manager who knows the local landscape keeps you out of trouble while you capture the upside.
