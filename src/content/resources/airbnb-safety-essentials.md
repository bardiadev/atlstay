A well-decorated, well-priced Airbnb that lacks the right safety equipment is a liability — for your guests, your reviews, and your business. Safety setup isn't a one-time task you do at launch and forget. It's an ongoing responsibility, and the properties that take it seriously earn the kind of trust that shows up in reviews and repeat bookings.

This guide walks through the core safety elements every short-term rental should have in place, framed as practical best practice rather than exhaustive legal compliance advice. Requirements vary by city, county, and property type — always verify what your specific jurisdiction requires with your local fire authority and building code office.

## Smoke Detectors: Placement and Maintenance Matter

Smoke detectors are the single most important safety device in any residential property. For a short-term rental, where guests are unfamiliar with the space, their proper placement and function is especially critical.

Best practice calls for a working detector on every level of the home, inside or immediately outside each sleeping area, and in the kitchen area (though kitchen detectors are often combination units to reduce nuisance alarms from cooking). Interconnected detectors — where triggering one triggers all — provide the fastest whole-home alert and are worth prioritizing.

Test detectors before every guest cycle if possible, and at minimum monthly. Replace batteries according to manufacturer recommendations, and replace units that are past their rated service life. A detector that chirps with a low-battery alert at 2am creates a terrible guest experience and often gets disabled — which is the worst possible outcome. ## Carbon Monoxide Alarms: The Invisible Hazard

Carbon monoxide is odorless and invisible, and guests have no way to detect it without a functioning alarm. Any property with fuel-burning appliances — gas stove, furnace, water heater, fireplace — or an attached garage needs CO detectors in sleeping areas and near any fuel-burning source.

Combination smoke/CO detectors simplify installation and ensure both hazards are covered in each zone. Like smoke detectors, they have a rated lifespan and need to be replaced when that period expires, not just when they stop beeping. Follow the manufacturer's specific placement guidance — CO detectors have height recommendations that matter.

## Fire Extinguishers: Accessible and Ready

Every rental should have at least one fire extinguisher, and it should be accessible to guests without hunting. The kitchen is the minimum placement; a second unit near any garage or mechanical room is solid practice.

Choose a multi-purpose dry chemical extinguisher rated for Class A, B, and C fires. Mount it visibly — not hidden in a cabinet. Check the pressure gauge indicator regularly and follow the manufacturer's inspection and service schedule. Extinguishers have a service life and should be inspected by a professional at the interval the manufacturer specifies.

Guests should be able to find the extinguisher intuitively. If it's mounted in a cabinet, label the cabinet clearly.

## Secure Locks and Entry Points

Short-term rentals benefit significantly from smart lock or keypad systems for the main entry. Beyond eliminating key logistics, they allow unique guest codes that expire at checkout — which means you're not relying on a previous guest returning a key. Look for a deadbolt-style smart lock rated for exterior use, and confirm the deadbolt engages fully when the door closes.

Secondary entry points — side doors, sliding glass doors, garage access — deserve equal attention. A sliding door without a secondary bar or pin lock is a weak point regardless of the quality of the main entry.

## First-Aid Kit and Emergency Information

A well-stocked first-aid kit in a clearly labeled, accessible location signals care and preparedness to guests. Restock after stays where items have been used, and review it periodically for expired items.

Alongside the kit, post a simple emergency information card somewhere visible — the property's street address (critical for 911 calls where guests may not know it), local emergency services numbers, and the nearest urgent care and hospital. Guests in an unfamiliar city are disoriented in an emergency; this information removes friction when it matters most.

## Clear Exits and Interior Signage

Every guest should be able to exit the property quickly in the dark, in a panicked state, without prior knowledge of the layout. Walk through your property with fresh eyes: are all exits easy to find? Are any blocked by furniture or clutter? Is there a clear path from each bedroom to an exit?

| Safety element | What to check |
|---|---|
| Smoke detectors | Present on every level, tested recently, battery current |
| CO alarms | Present near fuel-burning sources and sleeping areas |
| Fire extinguisher | Visible in kitchen, pressure indicator in green |
| Smart lock | Unique guest codes, secondary doors secured |
| First-aid kit | Stocked, labeled, accessible |
| Emergency card | Address, 911, nearest hospital posted visibly |
| Exit paths | Clear, unobstructed, navigable in low light |

For properties with stairs, consider clear lighting along stair paths. Night lights in hallways and bathrooms are inexpensive and reduce the risk of a guest injury that generates a claim and a damaging review.
