*This article is general information only — not legal, tax, or regulatory advice. Short-term rental rules change frequently. Confirm current requirements directly with the Unified Government of Athens-Clarke County before listing.*

Athens is one of Georgia's most distinctive short-term rental markets — a college town where demand concentrates sharply around the University of Georgia's calendar, particularly home football Saturdays and graduation weekends. That high-intent demand makes Athens attractive, and it also means the regulatory side deserves real attention: a property that performs on a game weekend still has to operate within the rules. This guide explains how that regulation works at a framework level; it avoids quoting specific fees, tax rates, or ordinance numbers, because those change and stating them wrong is harmful.

## One Government: The Unified Government of Athens-Clarke County

Athens has a feature that simplifies one part of the picture: it operates under a **consolidated city-county government**. The Unified Government of Athens-Clarke County merged the former City of Athens and Clarke County into a single authority, so for most of the area you are not navigating a separate city ordinance and a separate county ordinance — one government handles short-term rental regulation. That removes the "city vs. county" question that complicates places like Blue Ridge or Helen, but it does not remove the need to verify. Athens-Clarke County's framework is its own, distinct from Atlanta's and from any other Georgia community's, and it has been revisited in recent years. The authoritative source is the Unified Government of Athens-Clarke County, and confirming current requirements before listing is the right step.

(A couple of very small municipalities, such as Winterville and Bogart, sit within or at the edge of the county. If your property is in one, confirm whether it falls under the Unified Government or a separate town authority.)

## What the Athens-Clarke County Framework Typically Covers

Athens-Clarke County regulates short-term rentals locally, as Georgia communities do, and its framework has touched several areas owners should expect to address:

| Area | What it typically covers |
|---|---|
| Permitting / registration | Authorization to operate before listing |
| Zoning eligibility | Whether the use is allowed at a given address |
| Property classification | How owner-occupied vs. non-owner-occupied is treated |
| Operating standards | Occupancy, contact information, conduct |
| Taxes | State sales tax plus local lodging excise tax |
| Nuisance and parking | Limits that matter in dense, near-campus neighborhoods |

Each is set by ordinance and can be updated. Because Athens has revisited its short-term rental rules as the market has grown around the university, verifying the **current** requirements is especially worthwhile here.

## Zoning and the Near-Campus Reality

Zoning is where many Athens owners need to focus, because eligibility is not uniform across the county. Athens-Clarke County treats short-term rentals differently across zoning districts, and the neighborhoods closest to the UGA campus — exactly the ones with the strongest game-day demand — can draw the most regulatory attention, reflecting the recurring college-town tension between rental demand and the housing concerns of established neighborhoods. The practical consequence is simple: strong demand at an address does not mean short-term rental use is permitted there. Confirm zoning eligibility and any classification rules with Athens-Clarke County for the specific property before investing or listing.

## Taxes and HOA Covenants

Short-term rentals in Athens-Clarke County are generally subject to Georgia state sales tax and a local hotel-motel (lodging) excise tax, with the possibility of additional local requirements. Platforms collect and remit some of these in some places, but coverage varies and can change — do not assume platform collection equals full compliance. Confirm what you must file directly and the current rates with the Unified Government of Athens-Clarke County, the Georgia Department of Revenue, and a licensed CPA. Our [tax overview](/resources/short-term-rental-taxes-atlanta-owners/) explains the categories; the Athens specifics must be confirmed locally. This is general information, not tax advice.

Separately, a local permit does not override a condominium or homeowners' association that prohibits short-term rentals — near-campus condos in particular sometimes restrict the use, independently of any government permit. If your property is a condo or in a managed community, review the governing documents and confirm with the association before committing. Our [HOA rules guide](/resources/hoa-rules-and-airbnb-atlanta/) explains this layer.

The reliable sequence: confirm the governing authority (almost always the Unified Government, but check if you're in a small separate town); complete permitting before listing; verify zoning and classification at your specific address; clear any HOA or condo rules; settle taxes with the local government, state, and a CPA; and re-verify at least annually, especially before football season.
