A five-star review is won or lost in the first five minutes of a guest's arrival. Before they check the WiFi speed or test the mattress, they notice whether the place feels genuinely clean. A stray hair in the shower, a sticky counter, a coffee ring on the nightstand — any one of these can color an entire stay and turn a five-star experience into a four.

Turnover cleaning in a short-term rental is not the same as a standard house clean. It's a guest-ready reset that has to happen consistently, quickly, and completely — often with little room between checkout and the next arrival. The way to make it consistent is a checklist that leaves nothing to memory.

## Why a Checklist Beats Relying on Experience

Experienced cleaners miss things. Not because they're careless, but because speed and familiarity breed routine, and routine develops blind spots. The ceiling fan blade that hasn't been wiped in three turnovers, the microwave interior that keeps getting skipped, the shower grout that looked fine last time but doesn't now.

A written, room-by-room checklist solves this in two ways. First, it externalizes the standard — your cleaner isn't guessing what "clean enough" means, they're working through a specific list. Second, it gives you a shared reference when quality issues come up. Rather than a vague conversation about expectations, you can point to a specific item and ask whether it was completed.

## Kitchen: The Most Reviewed Room in the House

Guests who cook notice every detail in the kitchen, and guests who don't still use the counter, the coffee maker, and the fridge. The kitchen often drives the most specific comments in reviews — both positive and negative.

| Area | What to check |
|---|---|
| Counters and backsplash | Wiped clean, no crumbs, no sticky residue |
| Sink and faucet | Scrubbed, no food debris, no water spots |
| Appliances (inside and out) | Microwave interior, coffee maker carafe, toaster crumb tray |
| Refrigerator | Cleared of any previous guest items, interior wiped |
| Cabinets and drawers | Checked for stray items, handles wiped |
| Dishes and cookware | Clean, dry, and properly stored |
| Trash | Emptied and relined, no odors |
| Consumables | Coffee, dish soap, sponge restocked per your welcome standard |

The appliances are where turnovers most often fall short. A coffee maker with old grounds or a microwave with splatter inside will register immediately with guests — and show up in reviews.

## Bathrooms: Where Guests Set Their Review Expectation

Bathrooms are high-stakes. A bathroom that feels truly clean creates a baseline of trust; one that doesn't can undermine everything else the property does right. Every bathroom in the property needs the same attention, not just the primary.

Work top to bottom: light fixture and exhaust vent first (dust accumulates there), then mirror, then vanity surface and sink, then toilet (tank top, exterior, seat, bowl), then tub or shower (grout lines, fixtures, drain). Finish with the floor.

Restock toiletries to your standard quantities. Replace towels and the bath mat — even if they look clean, a fresh set signals care. Check the shower curtain liner for mildew. Confirm the toilet paper holder is full.

Hair is the single most common complaint in bathroom reviews. A final pass with a lint roller along baseboards and behind the toilet catches what the mop misses.

## Bedrooms: Where Guests Spend the Most Time

Linen quality and presentation matter here, but so does everything behind and beneath the furniture. Before making the beds, check under them — a sock or dust bunny under the bed is a guaranteed review mention.

Strip and replace all linens: fitted sheet, top sheet or duvet, pillowcases. Inspect pillows and the mattress protector — replace if stained. Make the bed neatly; it's the visual anchor of the room and the first thing in every listing photo.

Wipe nightstands, lamps, and lamp switches. Check the closet for any items left by the previous guest. Dust the ceiling fan. Confirm the TV remote has working batteries and that the TV actually turns on.

## Living Areas and Entry

The living room and entry set the tone the moment a guest walks in. Clear all surfaces, fluff and arrange cushions, wipe down the coffee table and remotes, and check for anything the previous guest may have left behind.

The entry specifically benefits from a quick once-over: welcome guide in place, keys or lockbox instructions correct, nothing blocking the path in. Guests who arrive to a visually clear, staged space feel the care immediately. Those who arrive to a crooked welcome mat and a jacket hook with someone else's umbrella on it feel the opposite.

## Final Walkthrough: The Quality Gate

Before leaving the property, do a room-by-room walkthrough as if you're the arriving guest. Turn on lights in each room, check corners and surfaces, open the refrigerator, smell the air. This two-minute pass catches the things that escape notice during active cleaning.

Take photos. A post-clean photo record protects you in disputes and creates accountability across turnovers — if something was in place when the cleaner left and damaged when the guest checked out, the photos establish the timeline.

## How Checklist Discipline Connects to Review Scores

Review scores are lagging indicators of turnover quality. A cleaning miss that earns a four-star review happened in the turnover, not during the stay. By the time you see the review, the next booking is probably already confirmed.

Managing to a consistent checklist is the operational lever that keeps cleanliness scores high without requiring you to personally inspect after every turnover. It also makes it much easier to identify and address individual cleaner performance issues before they accumulate into a pattern.
