Tybee Island is Georgia's beach — an 18-mile drive east of Savannah, small enough to feel like a genuine escape, close enough to the city to benefit from Savannah's year-round visitor draw. For short-term rental owners, that combination creates a market with real peak-season earning potential and a guest profile that differs meaningfully from Atlanta's urban rental market.

If you're an owner exploring what Tybee can produce — or already hosting and trying to sharpen your approach — understanding the local demand drivers, the property requirements, and Tybee's specific regulatory environment is the right place to start.

## Why Tybee Island Draws Visitors

Tybee's appeal is simple: it's Georgia's most accessible beach. For Atlanta families, the drive is under four hours and entirely avoids Florida's more crowded and expensive alternatives. For Savannah visitors, it's a short trip that adds a beach day or two to a city stay. That dual draw — regional beach destination plus Savannah overflow — gives Tybee a consistent base of demand that purely isolated beach towns don't have.

The island also has its own character. Tybee isn't a high-rise resort strip — it's low-rise, residential in feel, and leans into a laid-back beach town identity. Guests who choose it are typically seeking that experience: a porch, an outdoor shower, bikes, and a walk to the sand. The vibe matters to booking decisions here in a way it might not in a purely transactional hotel corridor.

## When Guests Book and Why

Summer is Tybee's clear peak. From Memorial Day weekend through Labor Day, beach demand reaches its highest point, and the compressed July Fourth and other holiday weekends can produce significantly elevated nightly rates for properties that are well-positioned. Families and friend groups booking annual beach weeks anchor the summer calendar.

But Tybee isn't purely seasonal. Savannah's event calendar — including the St. Patrick's Day celebration, which is one of the largest in the country, and a steady stream of weddings, film festivals, and food events — generates spill-over demand when Savannah hotels fill up. Spring and fall shoulder weeks are also growing as guests seek the beach without summer-peak pricing.

| Demand driver | Typical timing |
|---|---|
| Summer beach season | Memorial Day – Labor Day |
| Fourth of July weekend | Compressed high-rate peak |
| Savannah St. Patrick's Day | Mid-March |
| Spring break weeks | March – April |
| Savannah fall events and weddings | September – November |
| Holiday weekends (Memorial Day, Labor Day) | Long weekend compression |

## What Tybee Guests Want in a Property

Beach-market guests prioritize differently than city guests. The property features that drive bookings and strong reviews on Tybee are largely about outdoor living and practical beach logistics:

- **Walking distance to the beach**, or at minimum, easy car access without a complicated parking situation
- **Outdoor shower** for rinsing off sand — guests notice when it's missing
- **Screened porch or outdoor seating** — this is where beach guests spend their evenings
- **Adequate parking** for the group's vehicles — a meaningful booking factor on an island with limited street parking
- **Beach gear** (chairs, umbrellas, boogie boards) as a guest amenity — higher perceived value than their actual cost
- **Full kitchen and laundry** for longer-stay families who want to cook in during a week-long stay

Properties within a block or two of the beach command the strongest nightly rates. Homes that can accommodate larger groups — sleeping eight to twelve guests — tend to outperform smaller units during peak weeks, when families and groups make up the bulk of bookings.

## Tybee Island STR Regulations: Verify Before You List

This is important: Tybee Island operates under its own short-term rental ordinance, and that ordinance is notably more specific than what applies in many other Georgia markets. The rules govern permit requirements, occupancy limits, noise restrictions, parking requirements, and registration fees — and the ordinance has been revised multiple times in recent years.

Do not assume what applied previously still applies now, and do not apply what you read about Atlanta or other Georgia cities to Tybee. The City of Tybee Island is the authoritative source. Before listing, verify current requirements directly with the city.

## Pricing Across the Seasonal Curve

Tybee's seasonal concentration means a single flat rate is almost always wrong in some direction — either leaving money on the table during peak weeks or pricing out shoulder-season guests who represent real incremental revenue. The right approach treats different periods differently:

Peak summer weeks and holiday weekends should reflect compressed demand and limited available inventory. Shoulder seasons — particularly spring and fall — are better priced to drive occupancy at sustainable margins rather than sitting empty at peak rates that won't fill. Winter, when beach demand is minimal, can still produce bookings from guests tied to Savannah events or seeking off-season escapes at a discount.
