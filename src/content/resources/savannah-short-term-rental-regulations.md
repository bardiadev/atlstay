*This article is general information only — not legal, tax, or regulatory advice. Short-term rental rules change frequently. Confirm current requirements directly with the City of Savannah (and, for properties outside the city, Chatham County or the relevant municipality) before listing.*

Savannah is one of Georgia's strongest and most tourism-dependent short-term rental markets — and one of its most regulated. The regulatory side of a Savannah property deserves as much diligence as the revenue side. This guide explains how that regulation works at a framework level; it does not list specific fees or quote ordinance numbers, because those change and getting them wrong is costly.

## Savannah Regulates at the City Level

Like most Georgia communities, Savannah regulates short-term rentals locally rather than under a statewide law — but its ordinance is comparatively mature and detailed, reflecting how central tourism is to the city. In practice, a Savannah short-term rental sits at the intersection of several layers: **registration or permitting** before listing, **zoning and location eligibility** that varies by address, **operating standards** (occupancy, contact, conduct), and **tax** obligations. The specifics are set by the City and updated over time, and the authoritative source is the City of Savannah's planning, zoning, and revenue functions — not a third-party summary or a neighbor's recollection of what applied a few years ago.

## Location and Classification Matter More Here

Eligibility is not uniform across Savannah. The city has historically taken a location-aware, density-conscious approach, so whether you can run a short-term rental genuinely depends on the address and how the property is classified. Two distinctions matter most. First, **where the property sits**: eligibility has varied by area, and the Historic District and certain wards have drawn particular scrutiny. Second, **owner-occupied versus non-owner-occupied**: Savannah's framework has treated these categories differently, with different rules and availability. The single most valuable step a prospective owner can take is to verify a specific property's eligibility and classification with the City **before** buying or listing it. A property that looks ideal on a platform is not necessarily one the ordinance permits to operate.

## Chatham County and the Other Cities Around Savannah

"Savannah" is often used loosely for the broader coastal area, but the regulatory boundaries are precise. A property inside the City of Savannah limits follows the city's ordinance; one elsewhere follows a different authority — **unincorporated Chatham County**, **Tybee Island** (the beach community east of the city, with its own well-known rules), or **Pooler**, **Richmond Hill**, and other municipalities that each apply their own ordinances. Confirm which government actually has jurisdiction over your exact address before assuming Savannah's rules apply. For the beach market, our [Tybee Island guide](/resources/airbnb-on-tybee-island/) covers that distinct submarket, and Tybee's rules should be verified with the City of Tybee Island directly.

## Taxes, HOAs, and Historic Overlays: Separate Layers to Check

Short-term rentals in the Savannah area generally carry several categories of tax and fee — typically Georgia state sales tax, local hotel-motel (lodging) excise tax, and city-level lodging charges. Booking platforms collect and remit some of these in some jurisdictions, but coverage varies and can change. Do not assume that because a platform shows tax being collected, every obligation is covered. Confirm what you must file directly and the current rates with the City, the Georgia Department of Revenue, and a licensed CPA. Our [short-term rental tax overview](/resources/short-term-rental-taxes-atlanta-owners/) walks through the categories; Savannah's specifics must be confirmed locally. This is general information, not tax advice.

Separately, a City registration does not override a homeowners' or condominium association that prohibits short-term rentals, and it does not exempt a property from historic-district design and use restrictions. These are parallel systems, and any one can prevent a listing even when the others allow it. If your property is a condo, in a managed community, or within a historic overlay, review the governing documents and confirm with the association or review body before you commit. Our [HOA rules guide](/resources/hoa-rules-and-airbnb-atlanta/) explains how this layer works.

The reliable due-diligence sequence: confirm jurisdiction with property records; confirm the City permits short-term rental use at the specific address and how it's classified; complete registration before listing; clear the HOA, condo, and historic-district layers; settle taxes with the City, state, and a CPA; and re-verify at least annually, since Savannah's ordinance has evolved before.
